Notes

A couple of examples of canapés include: - Bouchée – which is a bite sized puff pastry canapé - Vol au vent – which is a large puff pastry canapé Canapés are traditionally served at the beginning of a French meal.

A similar equivalent in an English style service are “savouries” which are served at the very end of a meal. Canapés can be accompanied by an aperitif which is a drink served at the start of a meal.

Something such as Canapés Mephisto is still referred to as such even when served as a savoury as it is the proper name for the dish.

When working with pastry we discussed the proteins present in egg whites (and blood); albumins. When whipped the protein holds its shape as the protein strands lengthen and lengthen , therefore trapping air within themselves.

In a meringue, the sugar helps to retain this structure. You can also add a drop of something acidic to help stretch the proteins even further to create even more opportunity to trap air and make more bubbles.

Adding an acid to a pastry mix containing slackens the proteins in the flour and therefore helps it to stretch further without overworking the gluten, tugging and toughening it up, which causes the pastry to shrink during cooking.
