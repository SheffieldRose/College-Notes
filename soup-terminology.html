Soup terminology and notes

Stocks – There are two types of meat stock, white and brown. They are comprised of soluble flavourings, nutrients and gelatine derived from meat bones. The connective tissue in meat, such as the sinew and joints, contain collagen which renders down into gelatine but needs time to break down.

To make a white stock, using chicken bones, you start by washing the bones, or blanching them. Place into a fresh pan of water, then bring to the boil and skim. Then replace the water, boil and leave to simmer for around 2 hours, skimming it regularly until you have a clear white stock, flavoured by the chicken.

Brown stocks take considerably longer to achieve – taking around 8 hours to cook. The difference in technique here is that the bones are roasted off first, rather than blanched, in order to get Maynard reaction started (at around 50-150˚c) to impart a deeper flavour and colour into the stock.

Typical brown stocks may be made from veal or beef etc. An example being estaphade, made from beef and pork. If reduced by half, this stock becomes a demi-glace, which can be used as the base for many sauces, such as the sauce marchand de vin, made during our hot sauces module. If reduced by half again, this becomes a meat glaçe, which has a strength similar to that of a stock cube. This can be used as such, or as a flavouring in another sauce or as a glaze, such as the glaze we put on the parmentier potatoes that garnished the pheasant we cooked during year 1 of this course. A vegetarian alternative to this may be a yeast extract, which results in a similar product when glazing.

To make a fish stock takes significantly less time, using a white mirepoix, fish bones and white wine (along with other flavourings if desired). You start by completely lining your stock pan with butter, brushing the whole of the inside of the pan so that nothing sticks and starts to catch. This is to avoid producing a Maynard reaction and causing any browning or discolouration of the light stock. Once the pan has been lined, you sweat the mirepoix and fish bones lightly to get the process started. In with this, we put 1 bay leaf, 4 white peppercorns, parsley stalks, lemon juice, 100ml of white wine and 2 parts water. This only takes around 20 minutes to cook, any more and the stock begins to grey. Using this as the base for our fish soups, we also made a “fumet de poisson” which is a fish stock reduced by half, to have at hand in case of the need to impart more flavour into a stock. Taken further, a fumet becomes a glaçe, similar to a reduced meat glaçe and similar in its gelatinous texture.

You can make a vegetable stock by browning off the vegetables, a process called “dextronisation”.

All stocks are seasoned after cooking, so that the correct level of saltiness is maintained and not intensified during reduction as a result of evaporating liquid.

Consommé:

To ensure a consommé remains clear after the cooking process, anything acidic needs to go in during the initial process because otherwise it will continue to react with the proteins and coagulate in the dish making a cloudy spiral appear.

Purée soups are always served with croutons.

Cream soups are always served with their garnish.

Flutes sometimes used instead of croutons are baguettes that are cut on a bias and toasted.

Despumation in consommé production is the process of sitting a pan on a solid top with one half sitting on a hotter part so it boils and the raft goes over to the othe side of the pan (in an omelette shape). This is done so it can be skimmed more easily without breaking up the parts that have coagulated together.
