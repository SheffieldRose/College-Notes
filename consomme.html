To make a consommé, you need to clarify the liquid to be a nice, crystal clear, deep, black tea colour. Starting with a brown stock as described above , we used a mix of minced beef, chicken bones and egg whites to create a protein raft which we used to collect the impurities in the soup.

To ensure a more complex and enjoyable flavour in the consommé, we flavoured the stock by adding 1000g chicken bones, 250g mirepoix then removed it and cooled, then put 500g of the bones and all the mirepoix through the mincer with bouquet garni herbs , 8 peppercorns and 1 heaped tbsp tomato purée. Once we had done this, we added 5 egg whites to the mix and put this back into the stock stirring to ensure all the solids were evenly dispersed and suspended inside the liquid.

We then brought the consommé to a light simmer to achieve a process of despumation, whereby we placed one half of the pan onto a hotter part of the solid top than the other half so it boils and folds over onto the cooler half of the pan, rendering it easier to skim.

Once the raft had gathered all of the impurities in the stock and left it crystal clear, we skimmed all of the solids from the soup and passed it through a muslin over a chinois, then reboiled to ensure further clarity.

We served the soup in a large serving dish containing 4 portions worth (around a litre of consommé) of soup and presented with a julienne of swede, celeriac, carrot and green and white of leek, which we had blanched to stop from floating on top of the soup. We used beef bones, roasted off and simmered for 8 hours. We did this a week in advance and stored the stock in the fridge, then freezer to ensure we were prepared with the consommés ingredients on the day of making.

By mincing the beef, chicken bones and whisking the egg whites, the proteins in the mix become denatured – the state in which cooking processes such as emulsion or clarification begin. By denaturing the proteins, they open up and become prone to binding with other materials, in order to stabilise. In consommé, this is very visible in the formation of the raft.

While the minced mix is suspended and almost completely unbound in the consommé, it gradually groups together and forms a raft of solids on top of the liquid. During the process, the proteins gather everything up from the liquid to become part of this raft, and the liquid remains crystal clear, free from any floating pieces.

Herbs included in a bouquet garni are : thyme, parsley and bay leaf, although there may be variations. This can be made using a “plochette” which is a muslin pocket, or a “herb faggot” which is made of a leaf of leek folded around herbs.
